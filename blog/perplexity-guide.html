Perplexity AI Guide: The AI Search Engine Explained

A beginner-friendly guide · 4 min read

Perplexity is an AI-powered search engine that answers your questions directly, in plain language, and shows the sources it used. Instead of giving you a list of links to click through, it reads relevant pages and summarizes the answer for you — with citations you can verify.

It's especially useful for research, fact-checking, and getting quick, well-sourced answers without scrolling through multiple websites.

How is it different from a normal search engine?

  • It gives a direct written answer instead of just a list of links
  • Every answer includes numbered citations linking back to the original sources
  • You can ask follow-up questions in the same conversation, and it remembers context
  • It's better suited for research-style questions than simple navigation (like finding a specific website)

What can you use it for?

  • Researching topics for school, work, or personal projects
  • Getting quick summaries of current events or recent developments
  • Fact-checking claims by seeing the original sources
  • Comparing products, services, or options with cited information
  • Learning about unfamiliar topics with follow-up questions

How to get started

  • Go to the Perplexity website and create a free account (or use it without signing in for basic searches)
  • Type your question in plain language, just like you would talk to a person
  • Read the answer, then check the numbered citations to see where the information came from
  • Ask follow-up questions to dig deeper into the topic

Tips for better results

  • Ask specific questions rather than vague ones — "what are the side effects of X" works better than "tell me about X"
  • Use follow-up questions to narrow down or expand on an answer
  • Check the cited sources for anything important, especially for sensitive topics

Final thoughts

Perplexity is a great option whenever you need a quick, accurate answer with sources you can trust. It works well alongside traditional search engines, especially for research-heavy questions.
